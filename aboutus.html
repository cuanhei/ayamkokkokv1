KITCHEN

We always wash the ingredients before we started to chop

Washing ingredients before cutting or cooking is a fundamental practice in food preparation, driven by various essential reasons. Firstly, it's a crucial step in ensuring food safety by removing contaminants such as dirt, dust, and potential pathogens like bacteria or pesticides from the surface of fruits, vegetables, and other culinary components. This is particularly critical for items consumed raw or with edible skins. Moreover, the act of washing enhances the overall taste and appearance of dishes by eliminating unwanted residues that can lead to off-flavors and gritty textures. Additionally, washing helps mitigate the risk of cross-contamination in the kitchen, where the proper sanitation of hands, utensils, and ingredients is vital for preventing foodborne illnesses. Lastly, it aids in preserving the freshness of ingredients by reducing surface moisture, which can contribute to mold growth and spoilage. Different ingredients may require specific washing methods, emphasizing the importance of following food safety guidelines to maintain a clean and safe kitchen environment.

We always make sure the soup is hot before served

Firstly, temperature plays a pivotal role in enhancing the dining experience. Hot food, when served at the right temperature, allows flavors to fully develop, intensifying the taste and aroma of dishes. It also provides a comforting and satisfying sensation to diners, making their meal more enjoyable. Moreover, hot food and soups are often associated with safety and food hygiene. Cooking ingredients at sufficiently high temperatures helps kill harmful bacteria and pathogens, ensuring that the meal is safe for consumption. Restaurants prioritize food safety, and serving hot dishes is an integral part of that commitment. Additionally, certain cuisines and culinary traditions emphasize the appeal of hot, freshly prepared dishes. Whether it's a steaming bowl of soup on a chilly day or a sizzling plate of a favorite dish, the warmth adds to the sensory experience, creating a sense of comfort and indulgence that is deeply appreciated by diners. In summary, restaurants serve hot food and soups not only to elevate the taste and safety of the meal but also to cater to the emotional and sensory aspects of the dining experience, ensuring that patrons leave with a memorable and satisfying culinary experience.

FPlating food before serving is a fundamental practice in the culinary world, driven by several compelling reasons that collectively enhance the dining experience. Foremost, it's an artful endeavor aimed at creating dishes that are visually captivating. The arrangement of food on a plate allows chefs to showcase their creativity and attention to detail, transforming a simple meal into a work of culinary art. The aesthetics of a dish not only stimulate diners' senses but also set the stage for their culinary journey. Moreover, plating serves as a means of achieving balance and composition in a dish. Chefs meticulously arrange components to ensure a harmonious combination of flavors, textures, and colors. This deliberate arrangement leads to a more well-rounded and enjoyable taste experience, where each bite is a thoughtful blend of elements carefully curated to please the palate. Additionally, plating plays a practical role in portion control. By arranging specific quantities of ingredients on each plate, chefs can ensure that every diner receives an equal and satisfying portion of the meal, maintaining consistency in both quality and presentation. This attention to detail not only reflects professionalism but also ensures that patrons leave the restaurant with a memorable and gratifying culinary experience. In essence, plating food before serving is a culinary craft that extends beyond aesthetics; it's a form of expression, a quest for culinary perfection, and a means of delighting diners through both visual and gustatory pleasures.
